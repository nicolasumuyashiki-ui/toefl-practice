Quantum Entanglement and Information Theory

For most of the twentieth century, physicists treated the behavior of subatomic particles as a strange but largely separate domain from everyday physics. However, a growing body of work in quantum information has challenged this assumption by demonstrating that two distant particles can share correlations that no classical theory can replicate. These correlations, known as entanglement, do not transmit usable signals faster than light, but they encode information about each particle's state in a way that becomes apparent only when both are measured. Crucially, a quantum system holds information that is intrinsically distributed across two or more particles.

One landmark experiment, performed in the 1980s by Alain Aspect and his colleagues, used pairs of photons emitted in opposite directions and measured at separated detectors. The polarization measurements of the two photons remained correlated even when the detectors were far enough apart that no signal traveling at the speed of light could pass between them during the experiment. This finding suggests that the correlation does not arise from any hidden information carried inside the particles. Crucially, these results function as a form of distributed correlation, encoding shared information across the two particles' joint state rather than within either particle alone.

Critics have cautioned that many entanglement experiments rely on auxiliary assumptions about detector behavior and that closing every loophole simultaneously remains technically demanding. Nevertheless, the implications are profound: if information can be processed using these distributed correlations, then a future computer may operate through mechanisms beyond classical bits and binary logic alone.
