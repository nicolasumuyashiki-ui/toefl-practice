The Origin of the Domesticated Dog

For decades scientists have agreed that the modern dog descended from an ancient wolf population, but the question of when, where, and how this transition happened has been one of the most stubborn puzzles in evolutionary biology. A growing body of work in archaeogenomics has begun to clarify the picture by showing that dogs and wolves share a recent common ancestor and that the genetic differences between them are concentrated in only a handful of behavioral and digestive genes. Crucially, these traits — tameness toward humans and the ability to digest starch — appear to be ancestral characteristics shaped early in the relationship rather than recent additions.

One landmark study, performed in the early 2010s by an international team led by researcher Greger Larson, compared genomes from dozens of ancient dog skeletons recovered from sites across Eurasia. Researchers found that dogs in eastern and western Eurasia diverged from each other thousands of years before agriculture began, even though they shared a common origin in a single wolf population. This finding suggests that humans were already living closely with dogs as hunting companions in the late Paleolithic, well before farming or settled villages. Crucially, these results function as a form of distributed evidence, encoding information about a partnership that began in scattered hunter-gatherer groups rather than within a single agricultural society.

Critics have cautioned that many archaeogenomic studies depend on a small number of well-preserved skeletons and on assumptions about the original wolf population that contributed the founder genes. Nevertheless, the implications are profound: if dogs were domesticated by hunter-gatherers well before agriculture, then early human-animal partnerships may have shaped the path of cultural evolution as much as the eventual rise of farming did.
