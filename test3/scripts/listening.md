## Academic Talk 1 — talk1.wav (Q17-Q20)
**Topic:** How Volcanoes Influence Global Climate (Earth Science).
**Speaker:** Male professor (George, British accent).

> Today I'd like to step outside the small-scale picture of an erupting volcano and focus on something larger: the way major volcanic eruptions have repeatedly altered the global climate. We tend to think of volcanic eruptions as local hazards, threatening the towns and farmland nearby. But certain eruptions — what we call colossal eruptions — release such enormous amounts of material into the upper atmosphere that they can cool the entire planet for years afterward. The mechanism is actually quite elegant. During a major eruption, sulfur dioxide is injected high into the stratosphere, well above the layer where weather happens. There, it combines with water vapor to form tiny sulfate droplets, which scatter incoming sunlight back into space before it can reach the surface. Because these droplets sit above the rain clouds, they aren't washed out for a year or two. The result is a measurable, sustained drop in average global temperature.
>
> The classic case is the eruption of Mount Tambora in 1815. The following year, 1816, became known as the Year Without a Summer in Europe and North America. Frosts struck New England in June; crops failed across much of Switzerland and the Italian Alps; food riots broke out in several cities. People at the time had no way to connect these events to a volcano on the other side of the world, but modern ice-core records confirm the link. We see a clear sulfate spike in Greenland and Antarctic ice from that exact period, and tree rings from the same years show stunted growth right across the Northern Hemisphere.
>
> Now — and this is the point I want you to remember — these climate effects are temporary. After roughly two to three years, the sulfate aerosols settle out, and global temperatures rebound. Volcanic cooling is therefore very different from the long-term changes driven by greenhouse gases. Some researchers have proposed that we could deliberately inject sulfates into the stratosphere to offset global warming — a kind of solar geoengineering. The idea is technically plausible, but most climate scientists are extremely cautious about it, partly because the cooling is uneven and partly because, the moment you stop the injection, warming would resume even faster than before.

## Academic Talk 2 — talk2.wav (Q21-Q24)
**Topic:** The Economics of Behavioral Nudges (Social Science).
**Speaker:** Female professor (Charlotte, British accent).

> So far in this course we've assumed that consumers behave roughly like the rational agents in a textbook: they weigh costs and benefits, and they make the decision that maximizes their welfare. But there's a whole body of work, sometimes called behavioral economics, that asks: what happens when people don't behave that way? And more importantly, what can policymakers do about it without forcing anyone's hand? The answer is something economists call a nudge. A nudge is a small change in the way a choice is presented — it doesn't add new options, doesn't ban anything, doesn't even change the prices — but it shifts behavior in a predictable direction.
>
> The classic example comes from retirement savings. In a number of large workplaces, joining the retirement plan used to require an active enrollment form. Participation rates were low, and they stayed low even when the employer match was extremely generous. Then companies switched the default: instead of opting in to the plan, employees were enrolled automatically and had to opt out if they didn't want to participate. Nothing else changed — same plan, same fees, same investment options — but participation jumped from around forty percent to over ninety percent. People weren't making a different financial calculation; they were just no longer required to take an active step.
>
> A second example concerns organ donation. Countries that ask drivers to opt in to the donor registry have donation consent rates of around fifteen to twenty percent. Countries that use the opposite default — you're presumed willing to donate unless you opt out — have rates above eighty percent. Same population, same medical conditions, same general willingness to help others. The default is doing almost all the work.
>
> Now, nudges are popular partly because they are cheap to implement and partly because they preserve choice. Critics, however, raise two concerns. The first is paternalism: even if no option is removed, governments are still steering citizens toward outcomes that the designer prefers. The second is fragility: a nudge that works in one cultural context may not transfer to another, and once people become aware of the technique, the effect can shrink. So while nudges have become a standard tool of modern policy, they are best understood as a complement to traditional regulation, not a replacement for it.
