## Academic Talk 1 — talk1.mp3 (George) — Geology of Cave Formation
Q17=B, Q18=C, Q19=D, Q20=A.
> Today I'd like to talk about how some of the most spectacular underground spaces on Earth — limestone cave systems like those at Carlsbad in the United States or Mulu in Borneo — actually form. We tend to picture caves as dramatic, ancient features that just happen to be there. But geologically, most large caves are surprisingly young, and they form through a slow but very predictable chemical process.
>
> The basic ingredient is limestone, a sedimentary rock made primarily of calcium carbonate. Limestone is unusual in that it dissolves slightly in water that contains dissolved carbon dioxide. Rainwater picks up carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and from the soil as it filters down through plant roots, becoming a very weak carbonic acid. As that slightly acidic water moves through cracks in a limestone bed, it dissolves the rock along the way. Over thousands or even hundreds of thousands of years, what begins as a hairline fracture widens into a tunnel, and eventually into a cave passage large enough to walk through.
>
> The classic case study is the Carlsbad system in New Mexico. Geologists have shown that those caves were not carved primarily by surface water, but by sulfuric acid rising from below as petroleum deposits in the region released hydrogen sulfide. When the rising gas met groundwater, it produced sulfuric acid, which is far more aggressive than carbonic acid. That allowed Carlsbad's caverns to form much faster than typical limestone caves. Modern dating techniques applied to mineral formations inside these caves confirm a relatively rapid formation period of about four to six million years — short on a geological timescale.
>
> Now — and this is the point I want you to remember — once the cave is formed, the same process slowly reverses. Water dripping into the now-empty cave loses its carbon dioxide to the air and re-deposits calcium carbonate as stalactites and stalagmites. The same chemistry that hollowed out the cave now decorates it, drop by drop, over countless centuries.

## Academic Talk 2 — talk2.mp3 (Charlotte) — Economics of Public Goods
Q21=B, Q22=C, Q23=B, Q24=A.
> So far in this course we've assumed that consumers behave roughly like the rational agents in a textbook: they pay for what they want, and they don't pay for what they don't want. That logic works well for ordinary private goods like coffee or sneakers. But there's a whole category of goods that simply doesn't fit that model — what economists call public goods. A public good has two key features. First, my consumption of it doesn't reduce yours. If I look at a streetlight on a dark road, that light is no less available to you. Second, no one can easily be excluded from using it. Once a streetlight is on, the city can't realistically charge each passing pedestrian to look up.
>
> The classic example is national defense. If a country pays for an army, that army protects every resident, whether or not the resident contributed taxes. There's no practical way to defend only the people who paid. So defense is what economists call non-rival and non-excludable.
>
> The trouble with public goods is that the market tends to under-provide them. If a streetlight company tries to make a profit by installing streetlights, neighbors can simply enjoy the light without paying — economists call this free riding. Knowing this, the company doesn't bother installing enough streetlights, even though everyone agrees streetlights are valuable. That's why public goods are usually paid for collectively, through taxes or community contributions, rather than through ordinary markets.
>
> Now, public goods are often debated partly because the boundary is fuzzy. A toll bridge looks like a public good but is actually excludable — the toll booth excludes non-payers. A radio broadcast over the open airwaves looks like a public good and is, until the station encrypts the signal and turns it into a subscription service. Critics also argue that some goods now treated as public, like basic education or vaccinations, could be provided through private markets if rules were redesigned. The line between public and private good is therefore not fixed; it depends on technology and policy as much as on the goods themselves.
