The Bystander Effect

In 1964, a young woman named Kitty Genovese was attacked outside her apartment building in New York City. According to early reports, dozens of neighbors witnessed the event but none called for help. This shocking case led psychologists John Darley and Bibb Latané to investigate why people sometimes fail to act in emergencies when others are present.

Through a series of experiments, Darley and Latané identified a phenomenon they called the bystander effect. They found that individuals are less likely to offer help when other people are nearby. In one well-known study, participants heard someone in another room apparently having a seizure. When participants believed they were the only person aware of the emergency, 85 percent sought help. However, when they believed four other people were also listening, only 31 percent responded.

The researchers proposed that two main psychological processes explain this behavior. The first is diffusion of responsibility, which occurs when people assume someone else will take action. The second is pluralistic ignorance, in which individuals look to others for cues on how to react. If no one appears concerned, each person concludes that the situation is not serious. Together, these processes can prevent even well-meaning people from helping in critical moments.
